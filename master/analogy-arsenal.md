# Analogy Arsenal — Jimmy Rhoades

> Memorable metaphors and one-liners that consistently land in interviews.
> Use ONE per topic area per interview. Never repeat the same line twice
> in one conversation.

---

## Analogies

### F1 Racing
**Line:** "Make the car a little faster every week — stack small wins into momentum."
**Use for:** Performance measurement, continuous improvement, sprint velocity
**Context:** Explaining your approach to engineering productivity and iteration
**Landed in:** Ensemble R1 — Bethany connected personally (she and husband are fans). Created a bonding moment.
**Tip:** Works best with people who appreciate sports/competition metaphors. If interviewer seems more academic, use Cathedral instead.

### Cathedral / Brick
**Line:** "Hold the whole schema of the cathedral in your head and lay one brick at a time."
**Use for:** Balancing big-picture strategy with daily execution, roadmap planning
**Context:** How you drive business value quickly without losing architectural vision
**Landed in:** Ensemble R1
**Tip:** Pairs well with questions about speed vs quality. The cathedral IS the architecture; the brick IS the sprint.

### Software Deserts
**Line:** "$500K dev team can't solve $1,000 problem — AI changes that math."
**Use for:** AI strategy, identifying automation opportunities, where to apply AI
**Follow-up:** "Find where humans were inserted because automation was too expensive. That's where AI lives now."
**Landed in:** Ensemble R3 — Jesse engaged immediately
**Tip:** Very strong for companies building AI products. Makes the abstract concrete.

### Triage
**Line:** "Get rid of the murderers first, then the rapists, then the jaywalkers."
**Use for:** Prioritization, tackling technical debt, incident response, backlog management
**Context:** How you decide what to work on first when everything is urgent
**Landed in:** Ensemble R2
**Tip:** Colorful — reads the room first. Some interviewers love the directness, others may prefer a softer version. Safe alternative: "Put out the fires first, then fix the smoke detectors."

### Tokens / Thinking Big
**Line:** "If you're not running out of tokens, you're not thinking big enough."
**Use for:** AI adoption, innovation mindset, pushing boundaries
**Landed in:** Ensemble R2 + R3
**Warning:** Used twice in Ensemble R3 — diluted the second time. ONE use per interview, max.

### Intent Hierarchy
**Line:** "Values level: be kind. Business rules: keep retention below 5%. Operational: end calls in 3 minutes. Without the priority order, AI optimizes the wrong metric — 3-minute calls but customers churn."
**Use for:** AI governance, explaining why AI needs guardrails, system design with AI
**Landed in:** Ensemble R3 — Jesse said "interesting, I like it"
**Tip:** The call center example makes it immediately tangible. Always give the concrete example, not just the abstraction.

### AI Evolution Timeline
**Line:** "Pre-Thanksgiving it missed a lot, by December it looked pretty good, now we argue about taste — which is what developers always argued about anyway."
**Use for:** Demonstrating real AI usage, credibility as a practitioner not theorist
**Landed in:** Ensemble R3
**Tip:** Shows you've actually been in the trenches with AI, not just reading about it. The "taste" observation is the key insight.

### Wagon / Alignment
**Line:** "Everyone pulling the wagon the same direction."
**Use for:** Cross-functional alignment, managing competing priorities, stakeholder management
**Landed in:** Ensemble R2
**Tip:** Simple and universally understood. Good for leadership questions.

---

## Usage Rules

1. **One analogy per topic per interview.** Don't stack metaphors.
2. **Match the analogy to the audience.** F1 for culture-fit conversations, Cathedral for strategic discussions, Software Deserts for AI-forward companies.
3. **Track deployment.** If you used Tokens with the recruiter, don't use it again with the hiring manager at the same company.
4. **Let the interviewer react.** After delivering an analogy, pause. If they light up, let them respond. The best analogies create dialogue, not monologue.
