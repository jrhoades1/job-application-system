# Negotiation Frameworks Reference

Tactical patterns for specific negotiation situations. Referenced by SKILL.md.

## 1. Anchor First, Then Justify

Always state the number before the reasoning. "I'd like base at $225k" reads as
a firm ask; "Given market data, and the scope we discussed, I was thinking
maybe $225k" reads as a starting bid. First-number wins studies show the anchor
drags the final offer toward itself.

## 2. The "Aligned Incentives" Frame

When asking for a larger equity grant, frame it as alignment, not compensation:
"A larger grant now does more for keeping us aligned over 4 years than a higher
base ever could." This works because it sells the recruiter on the company's
own retention narrative.

## 3. Separate the Gap from the Ask

If the offer is $30k below target, do not say "I want $30k more." Say "Base
at $X keeps this aligned with comparable roles." The gap is not the ask. The
target is the ask. Pricing the gap invites a 50/50 split negotiation.

## 4. One Message Rule

One counter-email. Do not ping twice. Do not add an ask after they've
responded unless they opened the door. Every follow-up erodes leverage and
signals either indecision or escalation.

## 5. Silence Is Leverage

After sending the counter, wait. The worst play is a follow-up within 48
hours asking "any update?" That signals you'll settle. The second-worst is a
softening follow-up ("I'm still interested even if we can't get there").
Block out a week mentally.

## 6. Competing Offer Etiquette

If you have a competing offer, name the company only when asked. Frame it as
a constraint, not a threat: "I have another process that's moving toward an
offer at $X. I'd rather be here, but the gap needs to close for that to be
a real choice."

If you don't have a competing offer, don't fabricate one. Instead:
"I'm actively in process elsewhere" (true if in any late-stage interview) or
"I'm evaluating my options" (true always). Never lie about specific numbers.

## 7. Exploding Offer Tactics

If the offer has a <72h deadline without clear reason:

1. Name it. "I want to flag that the timeline feels tighter than usual for
   a role of this level. Can you walk me through the deadline?"
2. Ask for an extension before negotiating. Get to business week, minimum.
3. If they refuse, that's signal: they're either running parallel offers
   (backup plan) or testing compliance (culture tell). Decide whether to
   play along or walk.

## 8. The "Best and Final" Counter

When recruiter says "this is our best and final":

- Do not match their firmness with equal firmness on a different axis.
- Ask for the pricing rationale. "Which band does this map to? Can you share
  the level expectations so I can compare?"
- Silence. Let them justify.
- If justification is weak, one more ask on a different axis: "If base is
  truly locked, can we revisit the sign-on?"
- Accept the soft no if it's real. Don't burn the relationship.

## 9. Non-Cash Asks That Usually Work

When base is frozen:

- Sign-on bonus (especially to offset unvested equity at current employer)
- Start date flex (sometimes a month delay gets you a better relocation package)
- Relocation bump or temporary housing
- Annual refresh guarantee in writing
- First-year performance bonus target in writing
- Title bump at 12 months if milestones met (get it in the offer letter)

## 10. Declining Without Burning

If walking away, do it clearly and leave the door open:

```
Hi [Recruiter],

Thank you for the offer and for the time you and the team put into
the process. I've decided to go in another direction, but I want to
say — [specific thing you genuinely liked]. I'd be glad to stay in
touch, and if timing aligns again, I'd welcome that.

[Name]
```

Do not say why unless asked. Never complain about the comp in the decline
message. The company will be hiring someone similar in 6 months; the
recruiter has a long memory.

## 11. Accepting Cleanly

Accept in writing, confirm all key terms, and get the offer letter signed
before you resign:

```
Hi [Recruiter],

I'd like to accept the offer. To confirm the terms we discussed:

- Title: [X]
- Base: $[Y]
- Equity: [Z] shares, 4-year vest, 1-year cliff
- Sign-on: $[A]
- Start date: [date]

Can you send the final offer letter to countersign? Once that's in
hand I'll let my current employer know.

[Name]
```

## 12. After the Resignation, Don't Renegotiate

Once you've accepted and resigned, any further negotiation is with the
wrong leverage. Don't chase a late counter from the new company unless
you were explicit upfront that terms could still move.
