## Likely Questions & Executive-Level Answers

### "Tell me about yourself" / "Walk me through your background"
Use the PPF opener above. Don't go longer than 45 seconds.

### "Why Ensemble? Why Orchestration Works?"
> "Three things. First, the outcome-aligned revenue model — when automation improves collections, everyone wins. That alignment is rare. Second, the AI investment is serious: Mendel for clinical NLP, Cohere for agentic AI, and now a dedicated R&D division to execute. Third, I've built teams from zero three times — this is where I do my best work, at the intersection of greenfield engineering and real operational impact."

### "What's your leadership philosophy?"
> "Culture first, process second, scale third. My first hires are always the leads who set the tone — people who are collaborative, hold high standards, and make the engineers around them better. Then I establish engineering practices: testing as a first-class citizen, CI/CD with non-negotiable quality gates, and ownership of problems, not just tasks. Once the culture and processes are solid, scaling becomes a hiring problem, not a chaos problem."

### "How do you think about AI in a regulated healthcare environment?"
> "Deterministic foundation, AI enrichment. You need reliable, auditable rules-based systems as the backbone — and then layer AI where it creates value the rules can't capture. Every AI decision in healthcare needs to be logged, scored for confidence, and have a human escalation path. I've built this pattern before: at ilumed, we used ML for risk stratification on top of rules-based work list generation, all while maintaining HIPAA compliance and 99.9% uptime."

### "How would you work with the operations team co-located in Austin?"
> "They're the domain experts — they know the denial codes, the payer quirks, the workarounds that have evolved over years. My job is to translate their knowledge into systems. I'd embed them in the pod structure, have them in sprint planning, and make sure every feature maps to a real operational pain point. The best engineering teams I've built have always had that tight feedback loop with the business."

### "Tell me about a time you had to make a difficult leadership decision."
> "At ilumed, we inherited a legacy platform that was unreliable and couldn't scale. The team was attached to it. I had to make the call to modernize incrementally — not a big-bang rewrite — while maintaining production stability. We hardened the existing system first, built new capabilities alongside it, and migrated piece by piece. It was slower than some wanted, but we maintained 99.9% uptime through a 55% growth in beneficiary coverage. The lesson: earn trust by delivering stability before asking for permission to innovate."

### "What questions do you have about the role or organization?"
See "Questions to Ask" section below.

### "Where do you see yourself in 3-5 years?"
> "Leading a mature, high-performing engineering organization that's delivering measurable impact on Ensemble's revenue cycle. In 3 years, I'd want the A/R follow-up pod to be the model for how automation gets built here — strong engineering culture, tight partnership with operations, and provable business outcomes. Longer term, I'm interested in growing into broader engineering leadership as Orchestration Works scales."

### Risk-Check Questions (She's Looking for Red Flags)

**"What's your biggest weakness?" / "Where do you need to grow?"**
> "I can be too hands-on early in a project — I want to understand every system before delegating. That's an asset in the first 90 days when I need to learn the architecture, but I've learned to consciously step back once the team is established and trust the leads I've hired."

**"Why are you leaving your current role?" / "Why the move to Austin?"**
> "I've taken the company as far as I can in my current role, and I'm looking for a larger-scale challenge with a team that's investing seriously in AI. Austin is one of the two tech hubs I've had my eye on, and the opportunity to build from the ground up at Orchestration Works is exactly the kind of work I'm most energized by."

**"How do you handle conflict with peers or leadership?"**
> "Directly and early. I've found that most engineering conflicts come from misaligned definitions — what 'done' means, what 'fast' means, what 'quality' means. I start by aligning on definitions and success metrics. If there's still disagreement, I bring data. At Cognizant, I worked with security teams whose job was literally to say no — I learned to find the compromise that satisfies the security requirement without blocking delivery."
